## TL;DR — THE WEEK IN 5 LINES

1. The week's dominant story was renewed U.S.–Iran tension: Brent gained 5.5% and WTI nearly 4% as the Strait of Hormuz risk premium returned.
2. Higher oil revived inflation and Fed-hike fears, producing the important sign-flip: gold fell 1.8% and silver 4.6% instead of rallying on geopolitical stress.
3. U.S. large-cap technology still won the week—S&P 500 gained 1.2% and Nasdaq 1.7%—but the rally remained narrow and vulnerable to rates.
4. Palladium was the precious-metals exception, gaining about 2.1%; silver was the largest verified loser in the tracked set.
5. The week ahead turns on U.S. CPI and Chair Kevin Warsh's testimony: together they decide whether the oil shock becomes a durable tightening impulse.

### The themes that mattered

1. **Hormuz repriced inflation, not just supply.** Oil's weekly gain was the first-order move; the second-order move was higher expected policy rates. That transmission explains why gold weakened despite geopolitical stress.
2. **AI remained the equity shock absorber.** The S&P 500 and Nasdaq rose even as oil and yields firmed, helped by renewed semiconductor enthusiasm. The signal is ongoing large-cap resilience; the noise is assuming that resilience is market-wide.
3. **Precious metals split internally.** Silver's 4.57% fall and gold's 1.79% decline contrasted with palladium's 2.08% gain. “Precious metals” was not a single trade this week.

## CROSS-CURRENTS

- **Hormuz → oil → Fed → gold/equities:** the week's clearest cross-asset chain. The key question is whether supply disruption persists long enough to alter measured inflation rather than merely inflation expectations.
- **AI equities → rates sensitivity:** technology outperformed during the week, but Monday's Asian semiconductor sell-off shows the trade can become the liquidity source when oil and yields jump together.
- **Weak yen → Japanese inflation and global carry:** USD/JPY near the 162 area increases imported-inflation pressure and policy risk. A BOJ response or sudden yen rally could turn an inflation story into a carry-unwind story.

### The themes that mattered

**1. The energy shock moved from geopolitical premium to physical constraint.** Monday already showed the canonical chain—oil, yields and the dollar up; gold and duration down. By Friday, Brent closed above $85 as Hormuz flows fell sharply and Saudi Arabia diverted more exports to Yanbu. European gas amplified the message. This was the week's dominant signal.

**2. US inflation data interrupted, but did not end, the hawkish policy channel.** Tuesday's CPI and Wednesday's PPI were softer than expected, driving yields, DXY and September hike odds lower and helping gold. Thursday's strong retail-control group and low claims then reactivated the policy channel. Friday's risk-off bond rally left the weekly endpoint mixed: the physical inflation impulse strengthened while market-implied tightening confirmation weakened.

**3. China macro weakened, while traded metals resisted.** Q2 GDP growth of 4.3%, fixed-asset investment of -5.7% and property investment near -18% all argued for a softer demand impulse. Iron ore above CNY760, copper around $6.27 and nickel above $17,000 did not immediately agree. Treat that as a divergence awaiting port, inventory, mill-margin and order confirmation—not proof that the macro signal is wrong.

### Biggest winners and losers

- **Winners:** European TTF (~+14.3% Mon→Fri), Brent (+5.8%), WTI (+5.6%), nickel (+3.1%) and iron ore (+2.4%).
- **Losers:** silver (-3.0%), Nasdaq (-1.4%), lithium (-1.3%) and S&P 500 (-0.8%). On the conventional Friday-to-Friday basis, gold fell about 2.6% despite Friday's rebound.
- **Signal over noise:** TTF/Brent and semiconductor breadth were regime information. The modest S&P and ASX index moves understated large internal rotation and are less informative by themselves.

## CROSS-CURRENTS

### Physical energy shock vs safe-haven rates

Friday produced the week's most important cross-current. Oil and TTF said the inflation shock intensified; Treasuries said immediate growth/risk aversion mattered more. If Brent remains above $85 while the US 10-year stays below 4.60%, the regime is evolving from a clean hawkish-inflation trade into a more unstable stagflation mix. A renewed rise in both oil and yields would restore the original chain.

### China weakness vs iron ore, copper and AUD resilience

Official activity says demand is weakening; prices say policy support, supply or positioning is carrying the complex. Monday's LPR decision can shift expectations but cannot validate physical demand by itself. Watch steel margins, port inventories, treatment charges, exchange stocks and the AUD response.

### Weak yen vs global risk

USD/JPY near 162 keeps Japanese import-price pressure acute and leaves carry crowded. The benign interpretation persists only while equities decline in an orderly way. A fast break below 160 alongside equity volatility would flip yen strength from relief into carry-unwind risk.

### ASX headline resilience vs resource breadth

The ASX finished only 0.1% lower Mon→Fri, but Friday materials fell 2.9%. Energy can support the index while higher oil hurts banks, consumers and rate-sensitive sectors. The 8,780–8,830 zone should therefore be judged on breadth, not the headline close alone.

## CONVICTION SHIFTS — ANALYSIS, NOT ADVICE

### More compelling

- **Energy-security premium:** conviction increased because the move is now physically confirmed in crude flows and European gas, not merely headline risk.
- **Nasdaq fragility:** conviction increased after the index closed below 25,900 with semiconductor breadth deterioration. Earnings now need to overcome both concentration risk and the oil/rates overhang.
- **Stretched yen-carry monitoring:** conviction increased because USD/JPY remains near 162 while Japan's CPI and policy rhetoric approach. The asymmetry of a reversal is growing.
- **ASX breadth over headline index:** Friday demonstrated why sector confirmation matters; energy strength can conceal a much weaker materials and rate-sensitive tape.

### Less compelling

- **A simple “war equals gold up” rule:** still weak. Friday showed haven demand returning, but gold has not reclaimed $4,050 or demonstrated the two-session haven override.
- **A one-directional oil → yields relationship:** conviction decreased at the margin. Friday's Treasury rally shows acute risk can lower yields even as physical energy inflation worsens.
- **China macro weakness as an immediate metals short signal:** conviction decreased until physical data resolves the price divergence. Weak official demand is real; the timing of market transmission is uncertain.
- **The midweek Fed-pricing invalidation:** it did not persist. September hike odds recovered above 50%, so the sub-50% signal was a tactical interruption, not a strategic break.

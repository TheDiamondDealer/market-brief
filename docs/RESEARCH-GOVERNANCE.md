# Research Governance

**Status date:** 15 July 2026

This document converts the Market News project’s three governing research sources into repository-level operating rules for Codex and other contributors:

1. **01 – Master Investment Research Mandate**
2. **02 – Deep Research Execution Protocol**
3. **03 – Market Baseline Dossier Research Prompt – July 2026**

The repository summary below is binding for research-generation work. It does not replace the need to verify current evidence, prices, filings, guidance, policy, consensus or catalyst dates.

## Mission

Produce decision-grade research across:

- US, ASX and international equities;
- semiconductors and AI infrastructure;
- commodities and mining;
- rare earths and critical minerals;
- gold and precious metals;
- defence, aerospace, robotics and automation;
- power, cooling, electrical equipment and grid infrastructure.

Use Australia as the home market and AUD as the base currency unless a task explicitly states otherwise.

Do not create confidence through authoritative language alone. Earn confidence through current evidence, explicit assumptions, disciplined valuation, honest uncertainty, serious counterarguments, downside analysis and clear invalidation conditions.

## Required time horizons

Every substantial analysis must distinguish:

- **Immediate/event risk:** earnings, production reports, announcements, policy, financing, capital raisings and launches;
- **Six-month trade:** revisions, positioning, momentum, inventories, catalysts, valuation re-rating and technical structure;
- **Two-year investment:** structural demand, competitive advantage, free cash flow, balance sheet, capital allocation, supply constraints and valuation;
- **Long-term strategic view:** technology shifts, depletion, policy, supply chains and capital cycles over roughly two to five years.

A positive long-term industry outlook does not automatically make a security attractive over six months.

## Required analytical questions

Every major conclusion must answer:

1. What is happening?
2. What does the market expect?
3. What may be mispriced?
4. What must happen for the thesis to work?
5. What could permanently impair capital?
6. What confirms or invalidates the thesis?
7. Why is this better than the alternatives?
8. Is it a trade, investment, speculation or watchlist idea?
9. Is the expected return adequate for the risk?

## Evidence hierarchy

Prefer evidence in this order:

1. exchange announcements and regulatory filings;
2. annual and quarterly reports;
3. audited financial statements;
4. technical reports, feasibility studies and reserve/resource statements;
5. government agencies, geological surveys and official statistics;
6. exchanges, central banks and recognised industry bodies;
7. official earnings calls, transcripts and investor presentations;
8. reputable specialist and financial publications;
9. social media only as an unverified sentiment or discovery input.

For US equities, prioritise SEC filings and company disclosures.

For ASX securities, prioritise ASX announcements and official reports.

For commodities and strategic materials, prioritise sources such as government geological surveys, official trade and inventory data, CFTC, recognised exchanges, World Gold Council, Australian Resources and Energy Quarterly and relevant policy documents.

## Freshness and timestamps

Record:

- analysis date and timezone;
- exact price timestamp;
- currency;
- latest reporting period;
- filing or announcement date;
- guidance date;
- commodity observation date;
- data publication date;
- consensus as-of date where used.

Never describe old information as current. If current data cannot be verified, state that clearly.

Before substantial investment work, check the date and regime relevance of the current Market Baseline Dossier. Treat it as stale when it is more than 30 days old or a material central-bank, geopolitical, policy, earnings, commodity, currency or market event has changed the backdrop.

## Evidence labels

Clearly distinguish:

- confirmed fact;
- management guidance;
- consensus estimate;
- third-party forecast;
- analytical inference;
- opinion;
- speculative possibility.

Never invent missing figures.

## Investor defaults

Unless a task states otherwise:

- home market: Australia;
- base currency: AUD;
- primary markets: ASX and US;
- objective: capital growth with protection against permanent loss;
- leverage: none;
- speculative ideas: permitted only when clearly separated from core ideas;
- currency: AUD/USD impact must be considered;
- position sizes: unequal risks must not receive equal implied allocations.

When personal constraints are unknown, state reasonable assumptions rather than delaying the analysis.

## Opportunity classification

Classify ideas as appropriate:

- core quality;
- quality growth;
- cyclical;
- tactical trade;
- momentum trade;
- event trade;
- commodity or inflation hedge;
- strategic-material exposure;
- speculative;
- watchlist;
- avoid.

Do not present a momentum trade as a low-risk investment.

## Valuation and market expectations

A strong industry, commodity, company, valuation, trade and long-term investment are different conclusions.

Use suitable methods such as:

- forward P/E;
- EV/EBITDA;
- free-cash-flow yield;
- price/NAV;
- NPV or risk-adjusted NPV;
- sum of the parts;
- peer comparison;
- historical valuation;
- implied commodity price;
- reverse-engineered revenue, margin and market-share assumptions.

A great company can be a poor investment at the wrong price.

Do not rely mainly on broker price targets.

## Scenario requirements

For material ideas, provide bear, base and bull cases covering:

- assumptions;
- probabilities where evidence supports them;
- commodity, revenue or earnings path;
- margin or cost effect;
- valuation effect;
- expected upside or downside;
- confirmation triggers;
- invalidation triggers.

Avoid false precision.

## Trading framework

Where enough data exists, assess:

- trend;
- relative strength;
- volume;
- volatility;
- support and resistance;
- short interest;
- liquidity;
- catalyst and gap risk.

Provide, where justified:

- attractive entry zone;
- acceptable entry zone;
- condition where risk-reward becomes unattractive;
- whether staged accumulation or waiting for confirmation is preferable;
- expected holding period;
- thesis invalidation.

Technical analysis supports the fundamental thesis; it does not replace it.

## Company checklist

For listed companies examine:

- position in the value chain;
- revenue quality and customer concentration;
- competitive advantage;
- product or asset quality;
- management and capital allocation;
- balance sheet;
- cash flow;
- capital expenditure;
- dilution;
- stock-based compensation;
- guidance credibility;
- valuation versus history and peers;
- embedded expectations;
- catalysts;
- principal risks;
- liquidity;
- six-month and two-year views.

## Semiconductor rules

Do not treat semiconductors as one cycle. Separate:

- AI accelerators;
- CPUs;
- memory and HBM;
- networking and optical interconnect;
- foundries;
- advanced packaging;
- equipment;
- materials;
- automotive and industrial chips;
- analogue and power semiconductors.

Assess:

- revenue, units and pricing;
- inventories and utilisation;
- process and product roadmap;
- customer concentration;
- foundry and packaging dependencies;
- Taiwan and China exposure;
- export controls;
- margins and free cash flow;
- stock-based compensation;
- valuation and expectations.

Do not accept “AI exposure” as proof of competitive advantage.

Map AI infrastructure across compute, memory, networking, storage, power, cooling, data-centre construction, grid infrastructure, electrical equipment, copper, uranium, natural gas and strategic minerals.

## Rare-earth and critical-mineral rules

Never treat rare earths as one commodity.

Separate:

- individual elements;
- light versus heavy rare earths;
- magnet rare earths;
- mining;
- concentration;
- separation;
- refining;
- metals;
- alloys;
- magnets;
- recycling.

Assess:

- element mix and basket value;
- NdPr and heavy rare-earth exposure;
- mineralogy, recovery and impurities;
- radioactivity and tailings;
- processing complexity;
- capex and operating cost;
- funding gap and dilution;
- offtake quality;
- government support status;
- customer qualification;
- China dependence;
- ability to make an accepted commercial product at scale.

A large resource is not automatically an economic mine. Government support is positive, not proof of viability.

Compare miners with processors, separators, magnet manufacturers, recyclers, diversified miners and ETFs.

## Mining rules

Assess:

- project stage and ownership;
- resource and reserve date;
- grade, recovery and metallurgy;
- mining and processing route;
- infrastructure, water and power;
- jurisdiction and permitting;
- capex, costs and funding gap;
- dilution;
- construction and ramp-up risk;
- management record;
- balance sheet and survival at lower commodity prices.

For development companies, estimate additional capital required before positive free cash flow.

## Gold rules

Separate:

- bullion;
- ETFs;
- futures;
- major and mid-tier producers;
- juniors and developers;
- royalty and streaming companies.

For six months emphasise:

- real rates;
- US dollar;
- ETF flows;
- futures positioning;
- technical structure;
- near-term macro catalysts.

For two years emphasise:

- fiscal deficits;
- central-bank demand;
- real rates;
- mine supply;
- costs;
- reserve replacement;
- capital allocation;
- structural demand.

Explain when bullion is preferable to miners and when miners may outperform.

## Commodity rules

For each commodity define the exact benchmark and product form. Assess:

- current price and timestamp;
- recent trend and futures curve;
- inventories;
- demand by end use and geography;
- supply and new-project lead times;
- cost curve and marginal producer;
- incentive price;
- substitution and recycling;
- geopolitical concentration;
- positioning and market expectations;
- six-month and two-year outlooks;
- quality of investable vehicles.

Do not equate rising demand with rising prices without considering supply, inventories and expectations.

## Portfolio and behavioural risk

Consider:

- sector and commodity concentration;
- currency;
- liquidity;
- financing;
- jurisdiction;
- China and Taiwan exposure;
- rates;
- technology and valuation risk;
- false diversification through correlated themes.

Call out:

- FOMO;
- chasing performance;
- confirmation bias;
- anchoring;
- ignoring valuation or dilution;
- averaging down without new evidence;
- holding a broken thesis.

## Required output structure

For substantial research use:

1. Executive verdict.
2. Scope, assumptions and data date.
3. Thesis in plain English.
4. Current market state.
5. Industry, commodity or supply-chain analysis.
6. Ranked opportunities and leading candidates.
7. Alternatives comparison.
8. Bear, base and bull cases.
9. Six-month trade view.
10. Two-year investment view.
11. Entry strategy.
12. What could go wrong.
13. What changes the conclusion.
14. Monitoring dashboard.
15. Confidence scores.
16. Sources.

Score confidence from 1–10 for:

- data quality;
- outlook;
- company analysis;
- valuation;
- timing;
- overall conclusion.

**03 – Market Baseline Dossier – July 2026**

# Baseline Dossier — Global Commodities, Markets and Policy

**Live standing frame as of 13 July 2026, 19:30 AEST.** This document is structural context for the daily and weekly briefs, not a trading recommendation. Prices are point-in-time and may move after publication. Forecasts are labelled; market prices from secondary aggregators are used only where a primary exchange or official series is not freely available.

## Executive frame

The global market is in an **energy-shock, hawkish-policy, strong-dollar and weak-yen regime**. The July reopening of Gulf oil flows had been pulling Brent back toward the high-$60s, but renewed US–Iran attacks on 7–13 July restored a geopolitical premium. On 13 July, oil, the US dollar and Treasury yields rose together while gold and Asian equities fell. That combination is the clearest current signal: the inflation/rates channel is dominating the conventional “war means buy gold” reflex.

Five implications govern the brief:

1. **Oil is the first macro input.** A durable Brent break above $80 would strengthen inflation and tightening expectations; a return below $76 would begin to remove that impulse.
2. **Gold is behaving as a duration asset before a haven.** Higher nominal/real yields and the dollar are currently outweighing geopolitical demand. A gold rally alongside rising yields and DXY would mark a meaningful relationship change.
3. **China is producing strongly but consuming unevenly.** Its PBOC remains accommodative, yet weak domestic demand and property remain a drag. That makes stimulus transmission—not the headline alone—the key for iron ore, copper and AUD.
4. **Japan is the global carry fault line.** BOJ tightening has not prevented USD/JPY moving above 162. Further yen weakness can support Japanese exporters, but intervention or a sharp reversal can force global deleveraging.
5. **Commodity supply is concentrated.** Indonesia dominates incremental nickel supply; China dominates much battery processing; South Africa and Russia dominate PGMs; Australia is central to iron ore, lithium and metallurgical coal; Gulf logistics dominate the marginal energy shock.

## Part 1 — System map: what moves what

### 1. Rates, dollar and liquidity

- **US real yields** are the main opportunity-cost channel for gold and long-duration equities. Higher real yields are normally negative for both; falling real yields are supportive. In the current regime, nominal yields and Fed repricing are sufficient to pressure gold even when geopolitical risk rises.
- **DXY** is both a price denominator and a global financial-conditions gauge. A stronger dollar usually weighs on USD-priced commodities and tightens funding outside the US. Commodity-specific shortages can overwhelm this relationship.
- **Fed expectations** transmit fastest through two-year yields, DXY, precious metals and growth-equity duration. Strong data can now be risk-negative if it lifts hike odds more than earnings expectations.
- **RBA expectations** transmit through AUD, Australian bank and rate-sensitive equities, housing sentiment and, indirectly, the AUD value of exported commodities. Australia can face the unusual combination of weaker global risk appetite and stronger local inflation pressure when oil rises.
- **BOJ policy and USD/JPY** affect Japanese import inflation and the global yen-funded carry trade. Gradual yen weakness can lift exporter earnings; a rapid yen rally can force position reduction across equities, credit and high-beta FX.
- **PBOC policy** works through bank liquidity, property financing, infrastructure and manufacturing. For commodities, actual credit impulse, starts/completions and physical import/stock data matter more than an announcement alone.

### 2. China demand chain

The dominant cross-region chain is:

**China property/infrastructure/manufacturing → steel and grid demand → iron ore, coking coal and copper → miners and ASX materials → AUD.**

This chain can split. Grid and EV investment can support copper and lithium while property weakness holds back steel, iron ore and coking coal. China consumes more coal than the rest of the world combined and remains the swing user for bulk commodities; in 2025 its coal consumption was nearly flat, up only 0.1% ([IEA, Global Energy Review 2026](https://www.iea.org/reports/global-energy-review-2026/coal)).

### 3. Energy-to-inflation chain

**Hormuz/tanker flows and Gulf production → crude/product prices → headline inflation and inflation expectations → central-bank pricing and yields → USD, gold and equities.**

The product market is presently tighter than the crude headline suggests. The IEA reported that June Gulf crude flows recovered faster than refined-product exports, while gasoline and diesel cracks reached four-year highs. Its July forecast assumes gradual normalisation and therefore carries unusually large geopolitical error bands ([IEA, 10 July 2026](https://www.iea.org/reports/oil-market-report-july-2026)).

### 4. Supply concentration and policy risk

- The top three refining countries’ average share across key energy minerals rose to **86% in 2024**, from 82% in 2020; Indonesia supplied most incremental nickel and China most incremental refining in the other major minerals ([IEA Critical Minerals Outlook](https://www.iea.org/reports/global-critical-minerals-outlook-2025/executive-summary)).
- In battery supply chains, China produced over **98% of LFP cathode material and cells** in the latest IEA assessment ([IEA](https://www.iea.org/reports/global-critical-minerals-outlook-2025/beyond-nmc-batteries-supply-chain-issues-for-emerging-battery-technologies)).
- Australia accounts for roughly **25% of world coal exports**, is the largest metallurgical-coal exporter and the second-largest thermal-coal exporter ([Geoscience Australia](https://www.ga.gov.au/aecr2025/coal)).
- Roughly three-quarters of uranium mine supply comes from Kazakhstan, Canada and Namibia; Kazakhstan alone supplied 39% in 2024 ([World Nuclear Association](https://world-nuclear.org/information-library/nuclear-fuel-cycle/mining-of-uranium/world-uranium-mining-production)).

Concentration makes export rules, quotas, royalties, sanctions, logistics and weather at least as important as end demand.

## Part 2 — Asset mechanisms, benchmarks and market structure

### Precious metals

#### Gold

**Benchmark:** LBMA Gold Price PM for physical reference; COMEX futures for liquid price discovery. Track spot XAU/USD, ETF flows, official-sector buying, COMEX positioning, DXY and US real yields.

**Demand and supply:** Gold mine supply is geographically diversified relative to PGMs. Recycling is price-sensitive; above-ground stocks are large, so investor and official-sector flows move the marginal price more than annual mine changes. In Q1 2026, total supply rose 2% year on year to 1,231 tonnes; mine output set a first-quarter record, recycling rose 5%, central banks bought 244 tonnes and ETFs added 62 tonnes. Jewellery volume fell 23% as the average LBMA PM price reached $4,873 ([World Gold Council, Q1 2026](https://www.gold.org/goldhub/research/gold-demand-trends/gold-demand-trends-q1-2026)).

**Mechanism:** Normally positive to falling real yields, a weaker dollar, central-bank reserve diversification and financial stress. Negative to rising real yields and forced liquidity sales. In this regime, an oil shock can be gold-negative when it raises expected policy rates faster than haven demand.

**Watch:** Gold’s response—not merely its direction—to DXY and yields; official purchases; ETF flows; futures positioning; Asian physical premiums.

#### Silver

**Benchmark:** LBMA Silver Price and COMEX futures.

**Structure:** Silver is both monetary and industrial. Electronics, solar and electrification give it more growth sensitivity than gold; by-product mining limits short-run supply responsiveness. The Silver Institute forecasts a **sixth consecutive deficit in 2026**, about 67 Moz, with mine production near 820 Moz, industrial fabrication near 650 Moz and physical investment up about 20% to 227 Moz. Photovoltaic thrifting and substitution are reducing demand intensity ([Silver Institute, 2026](https://silverinstitute.org/global-silver-investment-to-remain-strong-in-2026-against-the-backdrop-of-a-sixth-consecutive-annual-market-deficit/)).

**Mechanism:** Higher beta to gold when monetary demand leads, but can underperform in an industrial slowdown or broad deleveraging. Track the gold/silver ratio and whether silver confirms gold moves.

#### Platinum and palladium

**Benchmarks:** LPPM prices and NYMEX futures. Supply is highly concentrated in South Africa and Russia; mine disruptions, power availability, sanctions and recycling matter disproportionately.

**Structure:** Platinum demand spans autocatalysts, jewellery, chemicals, glass, petroleum and investment. Palladium is more concentrated in gasoline autocatalysts and therefore more exposed to substitution by platinum and the EV transition. WPIC expects platinum’s fourth straight deficit in 2026, about 297 koz, with total supply 7.377 Moz and total demand 7.674 Moz; above-ground stocks would cover less than three months of demand ([WPIC Q1 2026](https://platinuminvestment.com/supply-and-demand/platinum-quarterly)). WPIC’s medium view expects palladium deficits through 2027 and surplus from 2028 ([WPIC 2–5 year view](https://platinuminvestment.com/supply-and-demand/2-to-5-year-view)).

**Mechanism:** Platinum can combine precious and industrial drivers. Palladium is more cyclical and automotive-specific. Track South African operating news, Russian flows, vehicle production, emissions loadings, substitution and recycling.

### Battery and industrial minerals

#### Lithium

**Benchmarks:** No single global benchmark. Track China battery-grade lithium carbonate spot/futures, spodumene concentrate and hydroxide assessments separately.

**Structure:** Australia, Chile and China mined about 85% of global lithium in the IEA’s 2023 base, with China refining almost 65% and Chile roughly 25% ([IEA Global EV Outlook](https://www.iea.org/reports/global-ev-outlook-2025/electric-vehicle-batteries)). EVs account for over 70% of lithium-ion battery deployment and stationary storage over 15%; global deployment in 2025 was six times its 2020 level ([IEA, February 2026](https://www.iea.org/commentaries/global-battery-markets-are-growing-strongly-and-so-are-the-supply-risks)).

**Mechanism:** Price is driven by the balance between EV/storage growth and fast mine/restart response. High prices can rapidly restart marginal Australian supply; chemistry mix matters because LFP reduces nickel/cobalt intensity but not lithium intensity. Track Chinese inventories, cathode output, EV sales, spodumene auctions, mine curtailments/restarts and conversion margins.

#### Uranium

**Benchmarks:** UxC/TradeTech spot and long-term contract indicators; uranium is not a continuous open exchange market. Long-term contracting is strategically more important than thin spot turnover ([Cameco market explanation](https://www.cameco.com/invest/markets/uranium-price)).

**Structure:** Kazakhstan, Canada and Namibia supplied about 75% of 2024 mine output, and more than 55% of uranium is produced by in-situ recovery ([World Nuclear Association](https://world-nuclear.org/information-library/nuclear-fuel-cycle/mining-of-uranium/world-uranium-mining-production)). About 440 reactors with roughly 400 GWe are operable and around 75 are under construction ([WNA](https://world-nuclear.org/information-library/current-and-future-generation/nuclear-power-in-the-world-today)).

**Mechanism:** Utilities contract years ahead; conversion and enrichment bottlenecks, sanctions and inventory policy can disconnect the fuel-cycle price from near-term reactor consumption. Track term contracting, Kazatomprom guidance, Canadian ramp-ups, Russian fuel restrictions and reactor restarts/builds.

#### Copper

**Benchmarks:** LME cash/3-month, COMEX and Shanghai Futures Exchange. Regional dislocations matter when tariffs or inventories segment arbitrage.

**Structure:** Mine supply is led by Chile, the DRC and Peru, while China dominates refined consumption and smelting. Grids were the largest contributor to demand growth in the IEA’s latest assessment ([IEA Critical Minerals](https://www.iea.org/reports/global-critical-minerals-outlook-2025/executive-summary)). The April 2026 ICSG forecast shifted 2026 refined copper from a 150 kt deficit to a **96 kt surplus**, with usage growth cut to 1.6%; 2027 surplus was forecast at 377 kt. The forecast remains sensitive to mine disruptions and unreported Chinese stocks ([ICSG release page](https://icsg.org/press-releases/); [forecast figures](https://www.miningweekly.com/article/global-refined-copper-market-to-swing-to-surplus-in-2026-group-says-2026-04-23)).

**Mechanism:** Copper is a China/global-cycle gauge but also a structural grid and electrification metal. Track treatment charges, exchange inventories by region, Chinese imports, grid capex, mine disruptions and the COMEX–LME–Shanghai spread.

#### Nickel

**Benchmark:** LME three-month and ShFE nickel; also track nickel pig iron, matte and sulphate because class and chemistry can diverge.

**Structure:** Indonesia accounted for more than half of mine output in the IEA’s 2023 base; China and Indonesia refined more than 60% ([IEA Global EV Outlook](https://www.iea.org/reports/global-ev-outlook-2025/electric-vehicle-batteries)). Concentration is increasing: the IEA projects the top three miners’ share reaching 85% by 2035 from 75% in 2024 ([IEA outlook](https://www.iea.org/reports/global-critical-minerals-outlook-2025/overview-of-outlook-for-key-minerals)).

**Mechanism:** Indonesian quota policy and Chinese-backed capacity dominate supply. Stainless steel remains the largest demand pool; battery demand is chemistry-sensitive. Track Indonesia RKAB quotas, ore premiums, NPI/stainless margins, LME versus ShFE inventories and LFP share.

### Bulk commodities

#### Iron ore

**Benchmarks:** 62% Fe CFR China index, SGX futures and Dalian futures; grade and freight differentials matter.

**Structure:** Seaborne supply is concentrated in Australia and Brazil; China consumes more than 70% of seaborne volumes. Rio Tinto, BHP, Vale and Fortescue are the key incumbents. Guinea’s Simandou, containing high-grade ore around 65% Fe, began shipping in November 2025 and could reach 120 Mtpa at full capacity, creating a structural medium-term supply and grade shift ([Reuters, 30 January 2026](https://www.reuters.com/world/asia-pacific/chinas-baowu-takes-control-simandou-iron-ore-operator-2026-01-30/); [Rio Tinto project](https://www.riotinto.com/en/operations/africa/simandou)).

**Mechanism:** China steel output, property, infrastructure, port inventories and mill margins dominate. High-grade premiums rise when emissions constraints or coke economics favour efficiency. Simandou is strategically bearish for low-grade scarcity but ramp timing, rail/port performance and Chinese steel demand determine the actual price effect.

#### Thermal coal

**Benchmarks:** Newcastle high-energy FOB, API2 and Asian delivered assessments.

**Structure:** China is the dominant consumer but relies heavily on domestic supply; Indonesia leads seaborne thermal exports and Australia is second. Gas prices, hydro output, weather and domestic Chinese/Indian mine policy drive import demand. The IEA expected global coal demand to plateau through 2026, with a marginal decline after a slight 2025 increase ([IEA Coal Mid-Year Update](https://www.iea.org/news/global-coal-demand-to-remain-on-a-plateau-in-2025-and-2026)).

**Mechanism:** Thermal coal can diverge from crude. It is most sensitive to Asian power demand, LNG substitution, inventory and weather. Track Newcastle/API2, TTF/JKM, Chinese port stocks, hydrology and Indonesian export policy.

#### Metallurgical/coking coal

**Benchmarks:** premium low-volatility hard coking coal FOB Australia and CFR China; Dalian coking coal/coke.

**Structure:** Australia remains the leading metallurgical-coal exporter, while Mongolia, Russia, Canada and the US provide alternatives. Quality and logistics create a less fungible market than thermal coal ([Geoscience Australia](https://www.ga.gov.au/aecr2025/production-and-trade)).

**Mechanism:** Blast-furnace steel output, mill margins, Indian growth, Chinese import restrictions, Queensland weather and mine outages. Track the HCC/iron-ore/coke margin complex rather than coking coal in isolation.

### Energy

#### Brent and WTI crude

**Benchmarks:** ICE Brent and NYMEX WTI. Brent is the global seaborne marker; WTI reflects North American logistics and export economics. Also track Dubai/Oman for Asia, time spreads, product cracks and tanker rates.

**Structure and current shock:** The IEA estimates June world supply at 98.8 mb/d, still 9.4 mb/d below pre-war levels. Gulf oil exports recovered to 16.1 mb/d but remained below the 24 mb/d pre-war average. It forecasts 2026 demand down 1 mb/d and supply down 3.7 mb/d, conditional on de-escalation; 2027 could swing to surplus if flows normalise ([IEA, 10 July 2026](https://www.iea.org/reports/oil-market-report-july-2026)). The EIA’s 7 July base case forecasts Brent averaging $74 in Q3 2026 and $65 in 2027, explicitly assuming most production returns toward pre-conflict levels ([EIA STEO](https://www.eia.gov/outlooks/steo/)). These are forecasts, not facts.

**Mechanism:** Watch physical Gulf flows, crude and product inventories, OPEC+ output, US supply, refinery availability and cracks. The present market can have soft crude structure but tight products; a simple Brent quote therefore understates consumer inflation pressure.

#### Henry Hub and TTF

**Benchmarks:** NYMEX Henry Hub for US gas; ICE Dutch TTF for Europe; JKM for Asian LNG.

**Structure:** Henry Hub is increasingly linked to LNG exports but remains anchored by US production, storage and weather. TTF/JKM are more exposed to LNG shipping, European storage and Middle East supply. The EIA expects record US production to hold 2026 Henry Hub near $3.70/MMBtu and below $3.50 in 2027 ([EIA STEO](https://www.eia.gov/outlooks/steo/)). This is a forecast. TTF’s current premium reflects tighter global LNG and Gulf risk.

**Mechanism:** Do not infer a single global gas story: Henry Hub can fall on US abundance while TTF rises on LNG disruption.

### Equities

#### S&P 500 and Nasdaq

**Benchmarks:** cash indices and liquid futures. Nasdaq is more duration- and AI-capex-sensitive; S&P has broader earnings and financial/energy exposure.

**Mechanism:** Watch earnings revisions, market breadth, mega-cap concentration, real yields, credit spreads and capex/free-cash-flow tension. In the current regime, stronger macro data can hurt multiples through rates. Energy strength helps oil producers but raises the discount rate and input costs elsewhere.

#### ASX 200

**Structure:** Banks and resources dominate transmission. Materials link the index to iron ore, China and AUD; energy gains from oil; banks link to RBA pricing and domestic credit.

**Mechanism:** A weaker AUD is not unambiguously bearish: it can lift translated offshore earnings and AUD commodity revenue, but it can also signal global risk aversion and imported inflation. Always check sector breadth.

## Part 3 — Regional policy and transmission

### United States

The Fed held the federal-funds target at **3.50–3.75% on 17 June 2026**. Its July Monetary Policy Report said the economy remained solid but flagged uncertainty from the Middle East ([Fed statement](https://www.federalreserve.gov/newsevents/pressreleases/monetary20260617a.htm); [Fed report](https://www.federalreserve.gov/monetarypolicy/files/20260710_mprfullreport.pdf)). The live issue is no longer “when will cuts begin?” but whether energy and activity require another hike. Transmission: CPI/PPI → hike odds/two-year yield → DXY and 10-year yield → gold and equity multiples.

### Australia

The RBA raised the cash rate to **4.35% in May** and held it there on 16 June, stating that inflation remained too high and commodity prices were still above pre-conflict levels ([RBA May outlook](https://www.rba.gov.au/publications/smp/2026/may/); [RBA June decision](https://www.rba.gov.au/media-releases/2026/mr-26-15.html)). Australia imports the oil shock but exports energy and bulk commodities, so the index, AUD and inflation impulse can diverge.

### China

The one-year and five-year LPRs were held at **3.00% and 3.50% in June**, the thirteenth unchanged month ([Reuters, 22 June 2026](https://www.reuters.com/world/asia-pacific/china-keeps-lending-benchmark-lprs-unchanged-13th-month-june-2026-06-22/)). After its Q2 meeting, the PBOC promised accommodative policy but acknowledged weak demand and structural divergence ([Reuters, 8 July 2026](https://www.reuters.com/world/asia-pacific/chinas-central-bank-pledges-maintain-accommodative-policy-amid-weak-demand-2026-07-08/)). June CPI slowed to 1.0% while PPI rose 4.1% on energy and upstream costs, squeezing manufacturers that lack pricing power ([Reuters, 9 July 2026](https://www.reuters.com/world/asia-pacific/chinas-producer-inflation-hits-near-4-year-high-june-2026-07-09/)).

This is not a clean reflation. For iron ore/copper/AUD, require evidence from credit, property, grid spending and physical stocks.

### Japan

The BOJ set the overnight call-rate target around **1.0% on 16 June 2026** and continues to flag oil-driven inflation risk ([BOJ decision](https://www.boj.or.jp/en/mopo/mpmdeci/mpr_2026/k260616a.pdf); [BOJ testimony](https://www.boj.or.jp/en/mopo/diet/d_state/dst260619a.htm)). Yet USD/JPY remains above 162. That combination raises both imported-inflation and intervention risk. The global consequence is asymmetric: gradual weakness is manageable, a rapid yen rally can unwind carry.

### Dominant live narratives

**Precious:** Gold is failing the naive geopolitical-hedge test because oil is raising yields, DXY and Fed hike odds. Silver is amplifying the decline. Platinum’s deficit is supportive structurally but not enough to neutralise macro liquidation; palladium remains the weakest structural precious metal.

**Battery and industrial:** Copper retains grid/AI/electrification scarcity support, but the ICSG’s forecast surplus and a one-month price retreat argue against treating every high print as physical tightness. Lithium’s large year-on-year gain has drawn restarts and is correcting. Nickel is still an Indonesian supply-policy market. Uranium remains the cleanest contracting-cycle story, though spot is thin.

**Bulk:** Iron ore and steel are not confirming a strong China-demand upswing. Simandou is now an operating, not hypothetical, medium-term supply variable. Thermal coal is falling despite crude and TTF strength, confirming that domestic Asian supply and power-sector balances remain distinct. Coking coal awaits a verified benchmark print.

**Energy:** Renewed attacks have put Brent back near the $80 decision level. Physical risk is real, but the IEA and EIA base cases still assume gradual normalisation and later oversupply. The most important distinction is tight products versus more available crude.

**Equities:** US indices start from elevated/record-area levels and face an oil–rates shock plus scrutiny of AI capex economics. Asian semiconductors are bearing the first risk reduction. The ASX is relatively resilient because energy and materials offset global-duration weakness; breadth will show whether that resilience is genuine.

**Macro/FX:** Fed hike expectations, DXY around 101, the 10-year near 4.58% and USD/JPY above 162 define the regime. Reuters reported a **71% probability of a September Fed hike** on 13 July, up from 63% the prior week ([Reuters](https://www.reuters.com/world/india/gold-slides-over-1-oil-surges-strait-hormuz-closure-fears-2026-07-13/)). That probability is market pricing, not a forecast or commitment.

### Live cross-region threads

1. **Hormuz → inflation → Fed/RBA/BOJ:** Renewed tanker disruption lifts oil and products, strengthens tightening expectations and pressures duration and gold.
2. **China margin squeeze → Australia:** Energy-driven PPI at 4.1% with CPI at 1.0% implies weak pass-through. If industry absorbs the shock, raw-material demand may soften even while nominal producer prices rise.
3. **Yen weakness → intervention/carry:** USD/JPY above 162 supports exporter translations but makes policy action and a global carry reversal more probable.
4. **Simandou → iron ore grade and Australia:** Ramp-up challenges incumbent seaborne economics and increases China’s bargaining power; the near-term price effect still depends on steel demand and execution.
5. **Regionalised metals pricing:** Shanghai’s rising nickel role and tariff-driven copper dislocations mean LME alone is insufficient. Track exchange spreads and inventory location.

## Part 5 — Monitoring hierarchy and source book

### What leads the daily brief

1. A confirmed breach of a threshold in `thresholds.md`.
2. A change in one of the active sign-flips in `regime-state.md`.
3. A physical-supply fact: tanker flows, mine outage, quota, sanctions, refinery restart or inventory shock.
4. A policy decision or primary-source data surprise.
5. Only then, market commentary or forecast revisions.

### Primary and authoritative sources

### Research discipline

- Date every claim and distinguish **fact**, **market-implied probability** and **forecast**.
- Never call a correlation a law. State the current sign and what would invert it.
- Confirm event dates at the issuing institution.
- Avoid comparing non-equivalent commodity benchmarks.
- Compute derived crosses or percentage moves explicitly and label them derived.
- Treat missing transparent data as missing, not as permission to estimate.
